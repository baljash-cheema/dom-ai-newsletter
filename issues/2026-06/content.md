Welcome to the inaugural issue of *AI in Medical Education*, a monthly briefing
from the Department of Medicine Education AI in MedEd Subcommittee. Each issue
takes one piece of AI scholarship and turns it into practical, ready-to-use
strategies for busy clinician-educators — no AI expertise required. This month
we open with a major *New England Journal of Medicine* review on how to
supervise trainees who are already using AI at the bedside.

## Article of the Month

**Educational Strategies for Clinical Supervision of Artificial Intelligence
Use.** Abdulnour RE, Gin B, Boscardin CK. *N Engl J Med.* 2025;393(8):786–797.
Review Article — Medical Education.[^1]

Large language models (LLMs) now generate fluent, confident-sounding clinical
reasoning, which makes them look more authoritative than earlier decision-support
tools and raises the stakes when a learner offloads real reasoning onto them.[^1]
This review argues that the central question is no longer *whether* trainees
should use AI, but how educators should **supervise** that use — and it offers a
structured way to do exactly that.

**Three named risks.** The authors describe *deskilling* (loss of a skill the
learner already had), *never-skilling* (failing to develop a skill at all because
AI was leaned on too early), and *mis-skilling* (learning the wrong thing because
an AI error or bias goes unchallenged).[^1] These risks are amplified because
LLMs behave as unpredictable "black boxes"; the review notes that in one study,
more than a third of advanced medical students failed to catch erroneous LLM
answers to clinical scenarios.[^1]

**The "AI interaction" as a teachable moment.** The review defines an *AI
interaction* as any moment a clinician receives AI output they cannot fully
retrace or verify — a point that should trigger a deliberate pause before the
output is trusted.[^1] Recognizing that pause is, in the authors' framing, the
foundation of critical thinking in the age of AI.

**A coaching framework — DEFT-AI.** Adapted from an existing Socratic precepting
model, DEFT-AI gives educators a short, structured script for debriefing a
learner's AI use in real time: **D**iagnosis & discussion, **E**vidence,
**F**eedback, **T**eaching, and an explicit **AI** engagement recommendation.[^1]

**"Verify and trust."** The review's closing argument is that critical appraisal
of *both* the AI tool and its output — borrowing the methods of evidence-based
medicine — must come *before* trust, not after it.[^1]

!!! note "Editor's Perspective"
    DEFT-AI is intuitive and well-grounded in established precepting theory, and
    its real strength is reframing AI use as a routine teaching opportunity rather
    than a violation. Three honest caveats for our faculty, though. First, it is a
    *proposed* model illustrated with a single fictional vignette — it has not yet
    been tested for feasibility or impact in real teaching settings. Second,
    running a full five-step debrief for every observed AI interaction may be hard
    to sustain under inpatient and clinic time pressure, and the review does not
    say how to triage which moments warrant it. Third, it places real new demands
    on educators — coaching AI literacy *on top of* clinical reasoning — without
    addressing the faculty development or protected time that would require. My
    recommendation: adopt the *posture* now (name the moment, ask how the learner
    verified the output), and treat the full script as a tool to reach for on
    higher-stakes cases.

**Put it into practice this month**

- **Name the moment.** When you see a learner consult an AI tool around a patient
  encounter, treat it as a teaching moment — the way you would a learner reaching
  for UpToDate or a calculator.
- **Ask how they verified it.** "What did you ask, and how did you check the
  answer?" surfaces both prompting skill and verification habits.
- **End with a recommendation.** Close with one of three: continue with
  supervision, continue independently with self-monitoring, or don't use AI for
  this task type.
- **Model your own verification.** When you use AI in front of learners, narrate
  how you check it — "verify, then trust" is one of the most powerful things an
  educator can demonstrate.

## AI Explainer — Centaur vs. Cyborg

A practical idea from this month's article worth teaching on its own. The review
describes two distinct ways people collaborate with AI, and argues skilled
clinicians shift between them depending on the task's risk and how well-validated
the tool is.[^1]

| Mode | What it looks like | Best suited for | Watch out for |
|------|--------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Centaur** | Learner and AI divide the work; the learner keeps the diagnosis and final decisions. | High-stakes, ambiguous, or diagnostic tasks; general-purpose tools not clinically validated. | Under-using a correct AI suggestion out of excess caution. |
| **Cyborg** | Learner and AI iterate together throughout the whole task. | Low-risk, well-defined tasks with validated tools (e.g., an ambient scribe drafting a note). | Automation bias and deskilling if used for high-stakes reasoning. |

The teaching point for trainees: the modes are not good or bad in themselves —
the skill is matching the mode to the task and keeping human judgment in charge
where it matters.[^1]

!!! caution "Pitfall of the month — automation bias"
    The review highlights *mis-skilling*: when clinicians were shown AI-generated
    predictions carrying systematic bias, they became more likely to adopt those
    incorrect conclusions themselves.[^1] The lesson for both trainees and
    faculty: an AI suggestion that "sounds right" is not evidence that it is.
    Treat AI output as a preliminary input to be checked against your own
    reasoning — not a verdict.
